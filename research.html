The Supply Chain of AI

When we talk about Artificial Intelligence, we often look at the output: the generated text, the recognized image, the solved problem. My research looks at the input. Specifically, I study the invisible human labor that powers modern AI systemsâ€”the "Supply Chain of AI."

Millions of workers on crowdwork platforms label data, train models, and verify outputs. My work investigates the fairness and ethical implications of these platforms. I am interested in how algorithmic management impacts worker well-being and how tasks are priced and estimated.

Current Projects

Procedural Fairness in Crowdwork
I am currently analyzing data from platforms like Prolific to understand how task duration is estimated. When algorithms underestimate the time a task takes, it leads to effective wage theft. I am using instrumental variable regression to isolate the causal impact of these estimations on worker behavior and platform fairness.

The Ethics of Digital Labor
Beyond the econometrics, I am driven by a question of dignity. As we build more advanced technologies, we have a responsibility to ensure that the human infrastructure supporting them is treated with fairness and respect.
