About Me

I study the human supply chain of Artificial Intelligence.

AI relies on invisible human labor. My research examines crowdwork platforms—the digital assembly lines where workers label data and train algorithms. I use econometric methods to understand fairness, task estimation, and worker retention in these marketplaces.

Before joining UCLA, I was the Head of Advanced Analytics at ACHS in Chile and a management consultant at Oliver Wyman. I hold an MBA from MIT Sloan.

Beyond the research and the data, my life is defined by my family. I am incredibly fortunate to walk this path with my wife, Andrea, and our four children: Carlota, Martín, Joaquín, and Juan Pablo. They are my greatest joy, my daily chaos, and my most important responsibility.

Research

Job Market Paper First Impressions Matter: Task Frictions and Retention on a Crowdwork Platform
Empirical Analysis Instrumental Variables Platform Design
Draft available upon request
Crowdwork platforms are central to the data supply chains that power AI systems, yet the dynamics of worker engagement remain poorly understood. Using a dataset of 64 million tasks, we analyze how early experiences—specifically procedural fairness and task underestimation—shape worker retention. Our results suggest that initial frictions substantially lower re-engagement rates, but those who remain have higher participation intensity.

Fairness in Crowdwork: Making the Human AI Supply Chain More Humane with Auyon Siddiq, Charles Corbett, and Catherine Hu Business Horizons (2025), Vol. 68, No. 5
AI relies on manual data annotation, often sourced through unregulated crowdwork platforms. Surveying workers in the AI supply chain, we find significant variability in perceived fairness and identify how platform design affects welfare.

The Impact of Information Systems on Experts' Decisions with Juan Pablo Atal, Jorge Ale-Chilet, and Alejandra Benitez Submitted
We analyze a system in a workers’ compensation program that flagged diagnoses with historically low coverage. While physicians denied coverage more often when alerted, they also evaded alerts by recoding diagnoses, suggesting the system acted through persuasion rather than information.
