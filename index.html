About Me

I study the human supply chain of Artificial Intelligence.

AI relies on invisible human labor: the workers who label images, annotate text, and teach algorithms right from wrong. My research examines crowdwork platforms, the digital assembly lines where workers label data and train algorithms. I use econometric methods to understand fairness, task estimation, and worker retention in these marketplaces.

Before joining UCLA, I was the Head of Advanced Analytics at ACHS in Chile and a management consultant at Oliver Wyman. I hold an MBA from MIT Sloan.

Research

Job Market Paper

First Impressions Matter: Task Frictions and Retention

with A. Siddiq, C. Corbett,
Empirical Analysis Instrumental Variables

Using a dataset of 64 million tasks, we analyze how procedural fairness and task underestimation shape worker retention. Results suggest initial frictions lower re-engagement but increase participation intensity for those who remain.

Publication

Fairness in Crowdwork: Making the Human AI Supply Chain More Humane

with A. Siddiq, C. Corbett, C. Hu

Business Horizons (2025). Surveying workers in the AI supply chain, we find significant variability in perceived fairness and identify how platform design affects welfare.

Journal Version
Working Paper

The Impact of Information Systems on Experts' Decisions

with J.P. Atal, J. Ale-Chilet, A. Benitez

Submitted to American Economic Journal: Applied Economics. We analyze an alert system in workers' compensation. Physicians evaded alerts by recoding diagnoses, suggesting the system acted through persuasion rather than information.

Request Draft
Working Paper

Searching for Serendipity

with I. Tucker, O. Sorenson
NK Modeling Simulation

Submitted to Strategic Management Journal. Does purposeful search kill serendipity? Using an NK modeling framework, we find that searchers with clear hypotheses are best at converting luck into value.
